Crate Pusher

After creating a time-continuous game, now felt like a good time to get into a time-discrete environment, and what better start than a puzzle game, about pushing crates obviously. Here, level design was the focus area. Making the most of the limitations your game environment to create challenging levels took a lot of trial and error. There’s a difference between a problem that’s difficult and a problem that’s challenging. Mainly, not every difficult problem is challenging. If your solution requires endless trial and error, that’s not challenging, that’s tedious. Using the lessons that you teach your players from previous puzzles in future puzzles is the key to rewarding gameplay.

Despite its apparent simplicity, this game taught me a lot about structuring your code. Those 'design patterns' I briefly mentioned in voxilent really started to appeal to me. Some observations: The observer pattern was all but crucial in the physics of both SPR and CP, the singleton pattern was overused and misused in almost every project, the prototype pattern was useful for creating the crate variants.

Additionally, data types and storage formats became of great significance. The proper separation of concerns for classes was a bigger priority than ever, and whist the scale of each game was decreasing the structure of each was steadily improving.
